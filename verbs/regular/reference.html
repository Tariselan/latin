Mood

With Latin verbs, mood is basically what kind of verb it is. Within Latin, we have four different moods. The Indicative mood - this is used for definite statements, think of it as the normal verb;
The Subjuncitve mood - this is used for uncertain statements such as wishes or conditionals (e.g. God save the king);
and The Imperative Mood - this is used for commands (this also tends to be the shortest form of the verb).

Voice

In latin we have two kinds of voice (active and passive). The active voice expresses what the subject is or does and the passive voice expresses what is done to the subject. ACTIVE: I killed Caeser. vs. PASSIVE: Caeser was killed by me.

Tense

There are six tenses in Latin. They're split into two groups, continued actions and completed actions. For Continued actions we have Present (I love, I am loving), Imperfect (I was loving, I loved), and Future (I will love). And for Completed actions we have Perfect (I loved, I have loved), Pluperfect (I had loved), and Future Perfect (I will have loved).

Person and Number

At the end of the verb, it often ends with what we call the person ending. Most of the time, the endings are: -m, -s, -t, -mus, -tis, -nt

What do the different parts mean?

Verbs change their endings to change their ending. The change in four ways: for Tense, Person, Voice, and Mood

Tense

In Latin there 6 tenses that we seperate into two groups. Continuous and 'Perfect' - Continuous tenses and Completed tenses. Continuous tenses talk about actions that happen over a period of time. I am eating (Present); I was running (Imperfect), I was running over a period of time; and I will explode (Future). Perfect tenses talk about completed actions. Caeser died yesterday (Perfect); He had fried three onions whole (Pluperfect) - the Pluperfect can be thought of a past in the past, so a thing that happened before the period of time (e.g. He had ran (Pluperfect) the day before, but the next day he swam (Perfect)); She will have pondered that (Future perfect).

Now completed and ongoing doesn't really make sense when talking about the future, so think of it instead from the perspective of the speaker / writer. Think of time in three sections: Past, Present, and Future. Now say an action will be going on when you arrive in the futre, that is when we use the Plain Future tense (He will carry). Compare this with an action that will be completed by the time you arrive in the future (He will have carried).

The tenses are also split into time periods. One in the Present (Present), two in the future (Future and Future Perfect), and three in the past (Imperfect, Perfect, and Pluperfect).

Person

In Latin basic verb sentences have a Subject (the noun that is 'doing the action'), a Verb (the action), and an Object (the noun being affected [optional]).
In lingsuistics, grammatical Person refers to (deictic) references in an event. We divide these into 3 catagories in Latin:

  1. 1st Person: The speaker - I (ego)
  2. 2nd Person: The Listener - You (tu)
  3. 3rd Person: The person or thing being talked about - He (is)
The end of the verb suffix always communicates the person. We distinguish between singular 1st, 2nd and 3rd and plural 1st, 2nd and 3rd.

Voice

Grammatical voice describes the relationship between the action (Verb) and the participants (Subject and Object(s)). In both English and Latin, we have two voices: Active and Passive. Take the following examples:

  • [ACTIVE] dominus servum docet - The master teaches the slave
  • [Passive] servus dominum docetur - The slave is being taught by the master

Mood

Grammatical mood describes what kind of event occurred. The indicative mood describes events that are factual statements.

Note that five of the tenses are compound tenses — formed using the passive perfect participle and the verb to be. The tenses are: Indicative Passive Perfect, Pluperfect, and Future Perfect; Subjunctive Passive Perfect and Pluperfect.
Also note that the verb to be takes the corresponding tense but active and continuous (e.g. perfect becomes present, pluperfect becomes imperfect, and future perfect becomes future).
