Cases

In Latin there are technically 8 cases: Nominative, Vocative, Accusative, Genitive, Dative, Locative, Ablative, and Instrumental. However the existence of Locative and Instrumental aren't used a lot so we tend to ignore them for the most part, so we'll focus on the following cases: Nom (Nominative), Acc (Accusative), Gen (Genitive), Dat (Dative), and Abl (Ablative).

The functions are as follows:

  1. 1. Nominative: Nominative is used whenever the noun is the subject.
    "rēx dormīvit" , "The king slept"
    "servus occidit rēgem" , "The slave killed the king."

  2. 2. Vocative: Vocative is used whenever the noun is being addressed.
    "christe, te laudāmus;" , "O Christ, we praise thee."
    "serve ī" , "Slave, go!"

  3. 3. Accusative: Accusative is used whenever the noun is the object or goal.
    "baculum porto" , "I carried the staff."

  4. 4. Genitive: Genitve means 'of'.
    "servus rēgis dormīvit" , "The king's slave slept"
    "servorum rāx puellae audīvit" , "The king of the slaves listened to the girl."

  5. 5. Dative: Dative is used whenever the noun is the indirect object ('to' or 'for').
    "servorumx rāx puellae audīvit" , "The king of the slaves listened to the girl."
    "feminae vir donum fācit." , "The man made the gift for the women."

  6. 6. Ablative: Ablative is the whatever case, if it doesn't fit into any of the other previous cases, it's probably the Ablative that'll be used. It tends to mean 'with', 'by', 'from', or 'in'.
    "Caecilius rēge rēxit." , "Caecilius ruled with the king."
